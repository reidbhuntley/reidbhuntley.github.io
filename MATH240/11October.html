Subspaces

Definition. Let VVV be a vector space and let H⊆VH\sube VH⊆V (HHH is a subset of VVV). We say that HHH is a subspace of VVV if all of the following are true:

  1. The zero vector 0 is in HHH.
  2. If u,vu,vu,v are in HHH, then u+vu+vu+v is in HHH. (Closure under addition)
  3. If ccc is any scalar and uuu is any vector in HHH, then cucucu is in HHH. (Closure under scalar multiplication)

H={0}H=\{0\}H={0} (the set only containing the zero vector) is always a subset of any vector space VVV, and VVV is always a subset of itself.

Is R2\R^2R2 a subspace of R3\R^3R3? No, because elements of R2\R^2R2 are in the form (ab)\begin{pmatrix} a\\b \end{pmatrix}(ab​), and elements of R3\R^3R3 are in the form (cde)\begin{pmatrix} c\\d\\e \end{pmatrix}⎝⎛​cde​⎠⎞​, which cannot be equal to each other; therefore R2\R^2R2 is not even a subset of R3\R^3R3, let alone a subspace. However, you can find subspaces within R3\R^3R3 that resemble R2\R^2R2.

Recall that C∞(R)C^\infin(\R)C∞(R) is the set of infinitely differentiable functions on the reals.
Is PPP, the collection of all polynomials, a subspace of C∞(R)C^\infin(\R)C∞(R)?

  1. Any polynomial can be differentiated as many times as you want, so PPP is a subset of C∞(R)C^\infin(\R)C∞(R).
  2. f(x)=0f(x)=0f(x)=0 for all xxx is a polynomial, so 000 is in PPP.
  3. The sum of two polynomials is also a polynomial, so PPP is closed under addition.
  4. The product of a scalar and any polynomial is also a polynomial, so PPP is closed under scalar multiplication.

Thus PPP is a subspace of C∞(R)C^\infin(\R)C∞(R).
