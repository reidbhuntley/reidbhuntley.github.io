Cofactors

Given an n×nn\times nn×n matrix AAA, let Cij=(−1)i+j detAijC_{ij}=(-1)^{i+j} \ \text{det}A_{ij}Cij​=(−1)i+j detAij​. The determinant of AAA now becomes
detA=a11C11+a12C12+...+a1nC1n.\text{det}A=a_{11}C_{11}+a_{12}C_{12}+...+a_{1n}C_{1n}.detA=a11​C11​+a12​C12​+...+a1n​C1n​. These CCC-matrices are known as cofactors of AAA.

Theorem: The determinant of a n×nn\times nn×n matrix AAA can be computed by a cofactor expansion
detA=aj1Cj1+aj2Cj2+...+ajnCjn\text{det}A=a_{j1}C_{j1}+a_{j2}C_{j2}+...+a_{jn}C_{jn}detA=aj1​Cj1​+aj2​Cj2​+...+ajn​Cjn​
or
detA=a1jC1j+a2jC2j+...+anjCnj.\text{det}A=a_{1j}C_{1j}+a_{2j}C_{2j}+...+a_{nj}C_{nj}.detA=a1j​C1j​+a2j​C2j​+...+anj​Cnj​.

This means the determinant can be computed across any row or column, rather than just the first row as shown previously. Because of this, we can speed up the expensive computation required to find the determinant by computing across a row or column with many zeroes.

Determinant of Diagonal Matrices

Definition. A diagonal matrix is a matrix where all of the non-diagonal entries are zero.

For a diagonal matrix AAA, the determinant of AAA is the product of its diagonal entries.

Ex.
A=(1000002000003000004000005)detA=(1)(2)(3)(4)(5)=120.\begin{aligned} A&=\begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0&0&0\\0&2&0&0&0\\0&0&3&0&0\\0&0&0&4&0\\0&0&0&0&5 \end{pmatrix}\\ \text{det}A&=(1)(2)(3)(4)(5)\\ &=120. \end{aligned}AdetA​=⎝⎜⎜⎜⎜⎛​10000​02000​00300​00040​00005​⎠⎟⎟⎟⎟⎞​=(1)(2)(3)(4)(5)=120.​
