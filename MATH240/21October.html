Unique Representation Theorem

Theorem. Let B={b1,...,bn}B=\{b_1,...,b_n\}B={b1​,...,bn​} be a basis for a vector space VVV. Then for each xxx in VVV, there exists unique c1,...,cnc_1,...,c_nc1​,...,cn​ such that x=c1b1+...+cnbnx=c_1b_1+...+c_nb_nx=c1​b1​+...+cn​bn​.

Isomorphism

Let B={b1,...,bn}B=\{b_1,...,b_n\}B={b1​,...,bn​} be a basis for VVV. Assigning coordinates to VVV gives a linear transformation from VVV to Rn\R^nRn that is one-to-one and onto. This means that VVV and Rn\R^nRn are isomorphic – they look different, but can be represented by the same mathematical object.

Ex. Let P5P_5P5​ be the set of all polynomials of degree five or less.
{1,t,t2,t3,t4,t5}\{1,t,t^2,t^3,t^4,t^5\}{1,t,t2,t3,t4,t5} is a basis for P5P_5P5​. This means any polynomial of degree five or less PPP takes the form P(x)=a5x5+a4x4+a3x3+a2x2+a1x1+a0P(x)=a_5x^5+a_4x^4+a_3x^3+a_2x^2+a_1x^1+a_0P(x)=a5​x5+a4​x4+a3​x3+a2​x2+a1​x1+a0​.
