Diagonalization Theorem

An n×nn\times nn×n matrix AAA is diagonalizable if and only if AAA has nnn linearly independent eigenvectors.

In fact, A=PDP−1A=PDP^{-1}A=PDP−1 where DDD is some diagonal matrix if and only if the columns of of PPP are nnn linearly independent eigenvectors of AAA. In this case the diagonal entries of DDD are eigenvalues of AAA that correspond to the eigenvectors in PPP.

What happens if a matrix doesn’t have nnn distinct eigenvalues?

Theorem.
Let AAA be an n×nn\times nn×n matrix whose distinct eigenvalues are λ1,...,λp\lambda_1,...,\lambda_pλ1​,...,λp​.

  • For 1≤k≤p1\leq k\leq p1≤k≤p, the dimension of the eigenspace for λk\lambda_kλk​ is less than or equal to the multiplicity of λk\lambda_kλk​.

  • AAA is diagonalizable iff if the sum of the dimensions of the eigenspaces is nnn. This occurs iff either of the following occur:

    1. The characteristic polynomial factors completely into distinct linear factors.
      (This is the case we dealt with before; this is true because each value’s eigenspace will have a multiplicity & dimension of 111)
    2. The dimension of the eigenspace for λk\lambda_kλk​ equals the multiplicity of λk\lambda_kλk​ in the characteristic polynomial.

  • If AAA is diagonalizable and BkB_kBk​ is a bsais for the eigenspace corresponding to λk\lambda_kλk​ for each kkk, then the total collection of vectors in the sets B1,...,BpB_1,...,B_pB1​,...,Bp​ forms a basis for Rn\R^nRn.
