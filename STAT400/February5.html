Total Probability

Definition. The event {A1...Ak}\{A_1...A_k\}{A1​...Ak​} is exhaustive if one of AiA_iAi​ must occur, i.e. S=A1∪A2...∪AkS=A_1\cup A_2...\cup A_kS=A1​∪A2​...∪Ak​.

The Law of Total Probability

Let {A1...Ak}\{A_1...A_k\}{A1​...Ak​} be exhaustive and exclusive (all of its members are disjoint with each other). Then, given another event BBB, P(B)=P(B∣A1)P(A1)+P(B∣A2)P(A2)+...+P(B∣Ak)P(Ak)=∑i=1kP(B∣Ai)P(Ai).\begin{aligned} P(B)&=P(B\mid A_1)P(A_1)+P(B\mid A_2)P(A_2)+...+P(B\mid A_k)P(A_k)\\ &=\sum_{i=1}^k P(B\mid A_i)P(A_i). \end{aligned}P(B)​=P(B∣A1​)P(A1​)+P(B∣A2​)P(A2​)+...+P(B∣Ak​)P(Ak​)=i=1∑k​P(B∣Ai​)P(Ai​).​

We implicitly used this rule to calculate P(B)P(B)P(B) in the previous example, because the three brands were exhaustive (you couldn’t buy any other brands of DVDs at the store) and exclusive (each DVD was made by only one brand).

Bayes’s Theorem

Using the same constraints on {A1...Ak}\{A_1...A_k\}{A1​...Ak​}, Bayes’s theorem states:

P(Aj∣B)=P(Aj∩B)P(B)=P(B∣Aj)P(Aj)∑i=1kP(B∣Ai)P(Ai).P(A_j\mid B)=\frac{P(A_j\cap B)}{P(B)}=\frac{P(B\mid A_j)P(A_j)}{\sum_{i=1}^k P(B\mid A_i)P(A_i)}.P(Aj​∣B)=P(B)P(Aj​∩B)​=∑i=1k​P(B∣Ai​)P(Ai​)P(B∣Aj​)P(Aj​)​.

Here P(Aj)P(A_j)P(Aj​) is called the prior probability of event AjA_jAj​, and P(Aj∣B)P(A_j\mid B)P(Aj​∣B) is called the posterior probability of event AjA_jAj​ given that BBB occurred.

Independence

Definition. Two events AAA and BBB are independent if P(A∣B)=P(A).P(A\mid B)=P(A).P(A∣B)=P(A). Otherwise, we say they are dependent.

The intuition for this is that knowing whether BBB did or didn’t occur doesn’t give you any more information about the chance of AAA occurring.
